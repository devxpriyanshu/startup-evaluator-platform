DeepTechEval — Functional Guide
Scoring · Valuation · Reporting · Decision support

Detailed explanation of how the platform works across scoring, valuation, reporting, and decision support.

1) Purpose of the Platform

This platform evaluates deep-tech startups in a structured, repeatable way for internal decision-making. It combines qualitative and quantitative inputs, computes module scores, calculates a 120-point total score, generates valuation estimates, and recommends an engagement path.

2) End-to-End User Flow

Page flow: Profile → M1 Tech → M2 IP → M3 Team → M4 Market → M5 Adoption → M6 Business → M7 Strategic Fit → Valuation Config → Review → Data Room → Report
  • User enters startup information across module pages.
  • Inputs are read by scoring functions for each module.
  • Total score and verdict are computed from module caps.
  • Valuation methods run using assumptions from Valuation Configuration.
  • Clicking Generate full evaluation report on the Review page, or the Generate report → button in the bottom navigation on the Data Room page, publishes the full report. The report shows a calculating placeholder for ~700 ms before rendering.
  • Generated report persists across tab switches until regenerated.
Navigation bar: A sticky bottom navigation provides Previous · Step X of Y · Methodology ↗ · Next →. The Methodology link opens the scoring-mechanism reference (the full 120-point scoring framework with per-field rationale) in a new tab. The bottom nav hides on the Report page since there is nothing further to navigate to.

3) Scoring Framework (120 Points)

Each module has a capped maximum score:

  • Module 1 - Technology readiness: 25
  • Module 2 - IP & patent assessment: 15
  • Module 3 - Team & leadership: 20
  • Module 4 - Market validation: 20
  • Module 5 - Adoption feasibility: 15
  • Module 6 - Business model strength: 15
  • Module 7 - Investor strategic fit: 10
  • Total: 120

The engine applies weighted transformations to sliders/selects (not only direct sums), then enforces module caps. Sub-weights within Modules 3 and 6 are rebalanced so the raw sum of fields equals the module cap — preventing a strong-on-every-dimension startup from being truncated by the cap.

Weight rationale UI: The Profile page shows a collapsible Why these weights? card. It explains why Technology and Team dominate (deep-tech failure causes), and justifies each module's relative weight with one sentence of framework reasoning. This makes the 25/15/20/20/15/15/10 allocation auditable rather than opaque.
Runway indicator (Module 6): A runway field — derived from reported monthly burn and cash balance — contributes up to 2 pts. Runway below 6 months scores 0, 6–11 months scores 1, and ≥12 months scores 2. This captures financial resilience independently of revenue maturity, so that a pre-revenue startup with healthy runway is not scored identically to one in survival mode.
FTO default: The Freedom-to-Operate selector defaults to Unknown → 1 pt, not 0. An absence of data is not the same as a declared absence of FTO analysis — evaluators must actively downgrade to 0 when the startup confirms no FTO work has been done.
M1 — Independent validation (binary, 1 pt): A dedicated field captures whether the technology has been validated by an external party — a published benchmark, third-party pilot result, accredited lab test, or peer-reviewed paper. This is reported separately from pilot status and empirical data quality, because external corroboration carries more diligence weight than any self-reported claim.
M3 — Team diversity (threshold field, 1 pt): Replaces the older Key-person dependency field. Awarded when the founding/leadership team passes a diversity threshold (≥ 3 distinct backgrounds across technical, commercial, domain, and geographic dimensions). Diversity is treated as a resilience signal — more routes to insight, fewer blind spots — rather than a compliance checkbox.
Sub-weight rebalancing (M3 & M6): Within Team and Business, the individual field maxima are reduced so that their raw sum equals the module cap exactly (20 and 15 respectively). Prior versions let field maxima sum above the cap, which truncated strong-on-every-dimension startups. Affected fields include Team Commercial experience (4→3), Industry network (3→2), Team completeness (4→3), Institutional origin (4→3), Advisory board (4→2), Execution track record (2→1), Full-time commitment (3→1); and Revenue model (8→3) in M6.

4) Verdict Logic (5 Bands)

Total score is mapped to decision-quality bands expressed as a percentage of the 120-pt cap:

  • Investment-grade — ≥ 85% (≥ 102 pts): strategically and commercially exceptional
  • Commercially ready — 75–84% (90–101 pts): ready to engage at full scale
  • Promising — 58–74% (70–89 pts): further validation required
  • High risk — 42–57% (50–69 pts): conditional support only
  • Not viable — < 42% (< 50 pts): reject or put on watch list

The additional Investment-grade tier separates truly exceptional opportunities from the merely commercially-ready band. It prevents top-decile startups from being under-signalled when the rest of the portfolio also scores in the 90s.

5) Engagement Decision Logic

Separate from score bands, the platform recommends one of five engagement paths, each gated by module-specific thresholds:

  • Equity investment — CRS ≥ 85, Strategic Fit ≥ 7, EPC Fit ≥ 3
  • Licensing agreement — CRS ≥ 70, IP ≥ 10, Strategic Fit ≥ 5, willingness to license confirmed
  • Engineering partnership — CRS ≥ 55, EPC Fit ≥ 3, Strategic Fit ≥ 4
  • Watch — 42% ≤ CRS < 55%, or upper gates not fully satisfied
  • Reject — CRS < 50, or Tech < 10, or Strategic Fit < 3 (hard floor)
Strategic investor hard gate: A technology scoring below 3/10 on Investor Strategic Fit is rejected regardless of how well it scores on the general framework. This enforces DeepTechEval's mandate — a strategic investor is not a generalist venture fund, and strategic alignment is a prerequisite, not a tie-breaker. License and Engineering recommendations additionally require Strategic Fit ≥ 5 and ≥ 4 respectively.

6) Valuation Engine (Configurable)

The platform can run multiple valuation methods in parallel:

  • Risk-adjusted DCF
  • VC Method
  • Strategic Value Uplift
  • Revenue Multiple
  • Berkus
  • Scorecard

Users can enable/disable methods and adjust assumptions such as discount rate, IRR, exit horizon, haircut, and sector revenue multiples. A Restore defaults action resets all valuation assumptions.

CRS-driven method recommendation: The valuation card highlights the preferred method based primarily on the total CRS score, with TRL as a secondary guard. Low-CRS startups are matched with qualitative methods (Berkus, Scorecard); mid-CRS with VC Method or Risk-Adjusted DCF; high-CRS with Revenue Multiple or Strategic Value Uplift. This forms a CRS → valuation bridge so that scoring and valuation do not drift independently.
TRL → WACC adjustment: The risk-adjusted DCF applies a TRL-driven premium on top of the user-entered discount rate, so early-stage technology is not discounted at the same WACC as a mature one. Premiums are additive: TRL 1–3 +10%, TRL 4–5 +7%, TRL 6–7 +4%, TRL 8 +2%, TRL 9 0%. Evaluators can therefore set a single base WACC and rely on the engine to stage-correct it rather than manually picking different rates per startup.

7) Reporting Experience

  • Report is intentionally generated only on explicit click for better UX.
  • A short calculating state (~700 ms placeholder) appears before report render, so users see a deliberate transition rather than a flash.
  • Generated report persists when switching tabs (top-tab navigation to the Report page does not clear it) until the user explicitly regenerates from Review or Data Room.
Two generate triggers: Reports can be published from either the Generate full evaluation report button on the Review page, or the Generate report → button in the bottom navigation of the Data Room page. Both run the same pipeline — computing scores, verdict, DWCS, valuation, and engagement recommendation — and land on the Report tab.

Report output includes:

  • Module breakdown bars and radar chart (each with an evidence-confidence badge)
  • Team/IP sub-indicator bars
  • DWCS panel — Dynamic Weight Calibration showing stage-adjusted module weights
  • Risk flags and risk matrix
  • Valuation cards, comparison chart, and CRS-driven method recommendation
  • Engagement decision cards with strategic investor hard-gate status
  • Portfolio snapshot table sorted by Priority Score

8) Saved Startups, Demo Data, and Delete Safety

  • Saved startups are stored in browser local storage under deeptecheval browser storage, with archived items kept in a local trash list.
  • Demo startups are seeded on first run and marked with a Demo badge for quick exploration.
  • Delete flow asks for confirmation and moves the item to trash rather than hard-deleting — so accidental deletes are always recoverable.
Undo window (30 s, live countdown): After any delete, a toast appears at bottom-right with a live-ticking countdown (updated every 250 ms) and an Undo action. The toast auto-hides after 7 seconds; the underlying undo window is 30 s. The toast uses white-space: nowrap so the countdown text never wraps awkwardly.
Trash retention (5 days, auto-purge): Archived items live in the trash for 5 days. On every page load and save, purgeExpiredTrash() silently removes items past that retention window. This prevents indefinite accumulation of stale evaluations while still giving a generous safety net.
Demo badge stripping: If an evaluator edits and re-saves a demo startup, the Demo badge is removed on save. Once edited, the row is treated as an original evaluation, not seeded demo content.

9) Data Room (Non-Scoring Evidence Overlay)

The Data Room is the last page before the report. It captures evidence metadata — things an evaluator wants recorded alongside a decision but that should not distort the 120-point score. Every Data Room field uses the vd-* id prefix and is excluded from the scoring engine.

  • Evaluation date, evaluator name, source documents reviewed — audit-trail metadata.
  • Peer comparables, industry benchmarks — context for the valuation range, not inputs to it.
  • Free-text notes — diligence observations, founder quotes, open questions, follow-ups.
Design rationale: Evidence and scoring are deliberately separated. If a comparables multiple drifted into the score it would double-count against fields like Market TAM/SAM; if an evaluator note became scored it would import subjective judgement into a framework whose value is reproducibility. Everything in the Data Room is preserved in the export/import JSON so the evidence trail travels with the evaluation.

10) Permanent Storage — JSON Export & Import

The tool is single-user and browser-local by design (no backend). To prevent loss when browser data is cleared or the user switches machines, saved evaluations can be exported to a plain JSON file and later re-imported — onto any modern browser, on any machine. Users typically save the file into OneDrive, Google Drive, or a USB/local folder.

  • Export all — downloads a single versioned document (deeptecheval-startups-YYYY-MM-DD.json) containing both the active list and trash.
  • Per-row export — a small ↓ icon on each saved row downloads just that one startup as its own JSON file, convenient for sharing a single evaluation with a colleague.
  • Import… — accepts a .json file, validates schema, and offers Merge (default, dedupe by id with later-updated wins) or Replace (wipe current state, load imported data verbatim — behind a second confirmation because it is destructive).
Schema v1.0: { schemaVersion, tool, exportedAt, fieldIdsSnapshot, savedStartups[], archivedStartups[] }. fieldIdsSnapshot records the complete field ID list at export time so future schema evolutions can detect drift during import and migrate safely.
Last-export reminder banner: The tool checks the last-export timestamp on load. If the last export was > 7 days ago an amber banner appears; > 30 days ago (or never, with ≥ 1 saved startup) a red banner appears. The banner is dismissable per-session and re-appears on the next session until an export refreshes the timestamp.
Out of scope: No backend server, no File System Access API auto-sync (Chromium-only), no encryption at rest. The JSON export is plaintext — if sensitivity is later flagged, an optional passphrase-based AES-GCM wrapper can be added without changing the export UI.

11) Methodology Reference Cross-Link

The sticky bottom navigation exposes a Methodology ↗ link that opens the scoring-mechanism reference in a new tab. That reference is the canonical source for the full 120-point scoring framework: every sub-indicator, its max points, its verbal anchor scale, and its inclusion rationale. When evaluators disagree with a computed module score, the Methodology page is where the argument is settled — not inside the evaluation form.

Colour system alignment: The three surfaces — the evaluation tool, the scoring-mechanism reference, and this functioning guide — share a single institutional palette (navy header, slate text, functional green/blue/amber/red for the five verdict bands). Colour is used to encode meaning, not for decoration. A reader moving between the three pages should feel a single system, not three disconnected tools.

13) Dynamic Weight Calibration (DWCS)

A startup at TRL 3 should not be evaluated with the same module priorities as one at TRL 8. DWCS applies stage-dependent multipliers to the seven module weights and re-normalises them to 120 points, producing a stage-adjusted score alongside the standard CRS.

  • Early stage (TRL 1–4): Team and Technology weights are emphasised; Business Model and Adoption are de-emphasised.
  • Mid stage (TRL 5–7): Market Validation and IP gain weight; the framework approaches the baseline allocation.
  • Late stage (TRL 8–9): Business Model and Adoption Feasibility dominate; Team weight is reduced since execution capacity is already demonstrated.

The report renders a DWCS panel showing baseline vs. adjusted weights per module, the adjusted CRS, and a one-line interpretation. Standard CRS remains the primary headline number — DWCS is a second lens, not a replacement.

14) Evidence Confidence Layer

Every module page exposes an evidence-confidence selector (High / Medium / Low). This annotates the score with a qualitative signal about how much the evaluator trusts the underlying inputs.

  • High: primary documentation reviewed — audited financials, independent FTO report, third-party pilot validation.
  • Medium: founder-provided data, single-source, plausible but not externally corroborated.
  • Low: verbal, unverified, or based on projections without evidence.

Confidence badges appear next to each module bar in the report and feed a cross-module indicator of overall diligence quality. A high-score/low-confidence profile is itself a risk flag — it prompts further investigation rather than action.

15) Portfolio Priority Score

Within the saved-startups portfolio, each entry gets a Priority Score used to sort the table:

Priority = 40% × CRS + 20% × Strategic Fit + 15% × Technology + 15% × Market + 10% × IP (all normalised to 0–100)

The weighting deliberately emphasises CRS and Strategic Fit to surface strategically aligned opportunities first, while Technology / Market / IP provide secondary differentiation inside the same CRS band. Priority is bucketed into High / Mid / Low and colour-coded in the portfolio table.

16) Practical Summary

In practice, the platform is a decision-support system that converts startup diligence into standardized scores, valuation ranges, and actionable engagement recommendations with transparent assumptions.
